Exponential Integral for Radiometric Altitude Corrections Reviewed

Adjusting counts of airborne gamma-ray spectrometric surveys to a consistent altitude is standard operating proceedure in exploration geophysics. In the lowest portion of the atmosphere, gamma-quanta counts decay nearly exponentially with alititude. To prevent the altitude of the survey platform from influencing the interpretation of the total counts, or from biasing the ground concentrations counts will be converted into, we do our best to adjust the measured signal to a common altitude. Ideally this will give relatively equal weight to the counts at each sample location.

How do we adjust the gamma-quanta counts measured at various altitudes to a common altitude? Well, very early on physicists observed that in the near surface environment, to first order gamma-quanta counts decay exponentially with distance from the ground. By observing quanta counts at various altitudes above a common source, we can estimate the sensitivity of the counts to altitude using an exponential function:

Eq. 1 N = N0 e-μx,

where N is the counts at altitude x, N0 is the counts at ground level, and μ is called the linear attenuation coefficient of air.

This model for altitude corrections is simple and effective. Notice just how simple it is. There are only two hyperparameters: N0 and μ. And just these two parameters do a nice job desribing a difficult problem: transport of high frequency photons through the lower atmosphere.

The first parameter N0 is the gamma-quanta counts at ground level. This can be measured in the field, or estimated based on various flight heights. It usually needs to be estimated, as will be pointed out later. The second paramter μ is almost always estimated. Thus the sensitivity of quanta counts to attenuation in the atmosphere can be turned into a two parameter optimization problem. In log-space, this could natually be posed as a linear overdetermined least-squares problem for log(N0) and μ.
